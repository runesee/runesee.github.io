Robotic Ocean Waste Removal: Ghost Nets

Robotic Ocean Waste Removal: Ghost Nets was an Experts in Teamwork project tackling the problem of locating, removing and modelling lost fish nets. My team's approach to this problem was an interactive web service that leverages data on reported lost fishing nets and uses ocean drift modeling (i.e., OpenDrift) to predict their locations. Lost fishnets are a global issue that cause significant harm to marine ecosystems. We tackled the modelling aspect of this problem; The goal of our solution was to "create a cost-effective solution that simplifies reporting and locating ghost nets, thereby incentivising their retrieval‚Äù.

TL;DR

An interactive web tool for reporting and tracking drift of lost fishnets.


On the landing page, already registered fishnets are fetched from the database and displayed in the frontend. Lost fishnets can be registered by clicking the corresponding button, and filling out the location and time of loss. For this proof-of-concept project, the application is geographically limited to the Norwegian coast.

Each net can be clicked, which opens up an information panel with further details about the net. Here, the drift path of the net can be simulated for 24 hours after its time of registration. If a simulation has already been computed, it is fetched from the database instead. Simulated drift paths contain a set of "particles" for each half-hour timestep. Each timestep can be viewed by adjusting the slider.


The heatmaps help visually showcase the uncertainty of the fishnets' drift. In the example simulation above, the net could have beached, or continued to drift north.

Depicted above is an architectural overview of the application. A key focus of this project was scalability. Down the line, we wanted it to be possible to simulate for longer periods, and, ideally, from anywhere around the globe. To achieve this, past simulations are stored, and redundancy is removed by rounding location and temporal data to reduce the number of unique simulations. Additionally, gathering data of lost fishnets from other sources is supported. While this proof-of-concept is duly limited, it demonstrates how modelling and reporting of lost fishnets could be combined through a single, global tool.
